## DIABETES

### What is diabetes?
Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disease characterized by elevated blood glucose levels. It occurs when the pancreas does not produce enough insulin or the body cannot effectively use the insulin it produces. There are three main types: Type 1, Type 2, and gestational diabetes.

### Type 1 Diabetes
Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system attacks and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. People with Type 1 diabetes produce little or no insulin and require daily insulin therapy to survive. It typically develops in children and young adults, though it can occur at any age. Symptoms include excessive thirst, frequent urination, unintended weight loss, fatigue, and blurred vision.

### Type 2 Diabetes
Type 2 diabetes is the most common form, accounting for about 90–95% of all diabetes cases. In Type 2, the body either doesn't produce enough insulin or doesn't use it well (insulin resistance). Risk factors include obesity, physical inactivity, family history, age over 45, prediabetes, and certain ethnicities. Management includes lifestyle changes, oral medications (like metformin), and sometimes insulin therapy.

### Diabetes Symptoms
Common symptoms of diabetes include: increased thirst (polydipsia), frequent urination (polyuria), extreme hunger (polyphagia), unexplained weight loss, fatigue and weakness, blurred vision, slow-healing wounds, frequent infections, and tingling or numbness in hands or feet.

### Diabetes Management
Diabetes is managed through: blood glucose monitoring, healthy diet (low glycemic index foods, portion control), regular physical activity, medications (metformin, sulfonylureas, insulin), regular medical check-ups, eye exams, foot care, and HbA1c monitoring every 3 months.

## HYPERTENSION (HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE)

### What is hypertension?
Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is a condition where the force of blood against artery walls is consistently too high (≥130/80 mmHg). It is often called the "silent killer" because it typically has no symptoms but significantly increases risk of heart disease, stroke, and kidney failure.

### Blood Pressure Categories
- Normal: Less than 120/80 mmHg
- Elevated: 120–129 / less than 80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: 130–139 / 80–89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: 140 or higher / 90 or higher mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis: Higher than 180 / higher than 120 mmHg (seek emergency care)

### Hypertension Risk Factors
Risk factors for high blood pressure include: age (risk increases after 55), family history, obesity (BMI over 30), sedentary lifestyle, high sodium diet, tobacco use, excessive alcohol consumption, chronic stress, and underlying conditions like kidney disease or sleep apnea.

### Hypertension Treatment
Treatment options include: lifestyle modifications (DASH diet, regular exercise, weight loss, reducing sodium to less than 2,300 mg/day, limiting alcohol), and medications such as ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers, diuretics (thiazides), and beta-blockers.

### Foods to Avoid with High Blood Pressure
Avoid or limit: table salt and high-sodium processed foods, deli meats and canned soups, alcohol (more than 1 drink/day for women, 2 for men), caffeine in excess, saturated fats (red meat, full-fat dairy), and added sugars which contribute to weight gain.

## COMMON MEDICATIONS

### What is ibuprofen and how does it work?
Ibuprofen is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) used to relieve pain, reduce fever, and decrease inflammation. It works by inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX-1 and COX-2) enzymes, reducing the synthesis of prostaglandins — chemicals that promote inflammation, pain, and fever. Common uses include headache, dental pain, menstrual cramps, arthritis, and fever. Side effects include stomach upset, GI bleeding (with long-term use), kidney strain, and cardiovascular risks.

### What are statins?
Statins are a class of cholesterol-lowering medications that work by inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase, an enzyme involved in cholesterol production in the liver. Common statins include atorvastatin (Lipitor), simvastatin (Zocor), and rosuvastatin (Crestor). They are used to reduce LDL ("bad") cholesterol and prevent cardiovascular disease. Side effects may include muscle pain (myopathy), elevated liver enzymes, and rarely, rhabdomyolysis.

### What is metformin?
Metformin is the most commonly prescribed first-line medication for Type 2 diabetes. It works by reducing glucose production in the liver, improving insulin sensitivity, and decreasing intestinal glucose absorption. It does not typically cause hypoglycemia and may support modest weight loss. Common side effects include nausea, diarrhea, and stomach upset, especially when starting treatment.

### What is acetaminophen (Tylenol)?
Acetaminophen is an analgesic (pain reliever) and antipyretic (fever reducer). Unlike NSAIDs, it does not have significant anti-inflammatory effects and is gentler on the stomach. It is used for mild to moderate pain. Overdose can cause severe liver damage — do not exceed 4,000 mg per day (3,000 mg for older adults or those with liver conditions).

## RESPIRATORY CONDITIONS

### What is asthma?
Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways characterized by recurrent episodes of wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and coughing. During an asthma attack, the airways swell, muscles around them tighten, and mucus production increases. Triggers include allergens (pollen, pet dander), exercise, cold air, respiratory infections, and smoke. Treatment includes quick-relief inhalers (albuterol) and long-term controller medications (inhaled corticosteroids).

### What is pneumonia?
Pneumonia is an infection that inflames the air sacs in one or both lungs, which may fill with fluid or pus. Causes include bacteria (most commonly Streptococcus pneumoniae), viruses (influenza, COVID-19), and fungi. Symptoms include cough with phlegm, fever, chills, and difficulty breathing. Diagnosis involves chest X-ray, blood tests, and sometimes sputum culture. Treatment depends on cause: bacterial pneumonia is treated with antibiotics; viral pneumonia is treated supportively.

## GENERAL HEALTH METRICS

### What is BMI?
Body Mass Index (BMI) is a measure calculated from a person's height and weight: BMI = weight(kg) / height(m)². Categories: Underweight (<18.5), Normal weight (18.5–24.9), Overweight (25–29.9), Obese (≥30). BMI is a screening tool but does not directly measure body fat or health. Limitations include not accounting for muscle mass, bone density, age, or distribution of fat.

### What are normal cholesterol levels?
Desirable cholesterol levels (mg/dL): Total cholesterol: less than 200 (borderline 200–239, high ≥240). LDL ("bad") cholesterol: less than 100 (optimal), 100–129 (near optimal), 130–159 (borderline high). HDL ("good") cholesterol: 60 or above is protective; below 40 (men) or 50 (women) is a risk factor. Triglycerides: less than 150 is normal.

### What is a normal resting heart rate?
A normal resting heart rate for adults is 60–100 beats per minute (bpm). Athletes may have lower resting heart rates (40–60 bpm) due to cardiovascular conditioning. Consistently above 100 bpm (tachycardia) or below 60 bpm (bradycardia) should be evaluated by a physician.

## PREVENTIVE HEALTH

### What vaccinations do adults need?
Key adult vaccinations include: annual flu shot, COVID-19 vaccines (as recommended), Tdap (tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis) every 10 years, pneumococcal vaccines (for adults 65+), shingles vaccine (Shingrix, ages 50+), and HPV vaccine (through age 26, some through 45). Consult your healthcare provider for personalized recommendations.

### How much sleep do adults need?
Adults generally need 7–9 hours of sleep per night. Insufficient sleep is linked to increased risk of obesity, heart disease, diabetes, depression, and reduced immune function. Sleep hygiene tips include: consistent sleep schedule, limiting screen time before bed, avoiding caffeine after 2 PM, and keeping the bedroom cool and dark.

### How much physical activity is recommended?
The CDC recommends adults get: at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity (e.g., brisk walking) per week OR 75 minutes of vigorous activity (e.g., running), plus muscle-strengthening activities (e.g., weightlifting) on 2 or more days per week. Regular physical activity reduces risk of heart disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, depression, and certain cancers.
